Why AI Recommends Some Wineries by Name — and Never Mentions Yours

When a buyer asks ChatGPT for a great bottle, some wineries get named and others vanish. Here’s what separates them — and how to get on the right side of it.

Picture a sommelier building a by-the-glass list, a shop buyer hunting for something new, or a collector chasing an allocation. A few years ago they’d Google it, call a rep, or ask around. Today, a growing number of them open ChatGPT and type something like “recommend a small-production Lodi Zinfandel” or “which Napa wineries still sell allocations to the public?” The AI thinks for a second and names a handful of wineries.

If yours is one of them, you just made a shortlist you didn’t know existed. If it isn’t, you were never in the room. This is happening in the background, all day — and it has almost nothing to do with how good your wine is.

The machine can’t taste your wine

The AI has never tasted your wine and never will. It builds its answer from what it can read about you across the internet — your site, your listings, your reviews, your press. So the wineries that get named aren’t necessarily the best in the glass. They’re the ones easiest for a machine to understand. A gorgeous site with a full-screen vineyard video and barely any words tells an AI almost nothing. A plainer page that clearly says what you grow, where, and why gets quoted.

What separates the named from the invisible

1. Don’t trap your words in pictures

Wineries love to put tasting notes, tech sheets, even their whole story inside images and downloadable PDFs. They look beautiful — and they’re nearly invisible to AI, which reads text far better than pictures. If your Cabernet’s tasting note lives only inside a JPEG or a PDF, as far as the machine is concerned it doesn’t exist. Put the words on the page, as real text.

2. Answer the questions people actually ask

Buyers and drinkers ask specific things: What’s the appellation? What’s the blend? Is it organic or dry-farmed? How do allocations work? Can I visit? Wineries that answer those plainly — in writing, on the page — hand AI clean, quotable passages. A real FAQ, honest tasting notes, a clear “plan your visit” page, a “how our wine club works” page. Poetic, vague copy is lovely for humans and useless to a machine trying to answer a specific question.

3. One name, everywhere

Your winery name, your label brands, your DBA, your appellation, your address — they need to match across your site, Vivino, Wine-Searcher, CellarTracker, Google Business, and your distributors’ listings. When “Smith Family Vineyards,” “Smith Family Winery,” and “Smith Cellars” all float around, you teach the machines you’re three faint things instead of one trusted one.

4. Tell the machines what you are

There’s a bit of invisible code — structured data — that labels your site as a winery, your bottles as products, your reviews as reviews. Most winery sites, especially the prettiest ones, skip it entirely. Adding it is how you become eligible for the richer Google results (ratings, FAQs) and how an AI restates your details accurately instead of guessing.

5. Make sure you’re not locked out

Some winery sites — often the ones built on drag-and-drop platforms, or gated behind an age-check that hides everything — accidentally shut out the very crawlers that feed AI answers. If the bots can’t read your page, you can never be recommended. It’s worth checking.

6. Earn authority the wine way

AI leans on sources it already trusts. Coverage in Wine Enthusiast, Wine Business Monthly, or your local press; a healthy presence on Vivino and Wine-Searcher; genuine reviews — these are what tell the machine you’re real and worth naming.

I didn’t just theorize this

I built a tool called COLAClear that checks alcohol labels against federal rules, and I ran this exact playbook on it. Within months, with zero ad spend, Google’s AI Overview started citing COLAClear by name as the tool for the job. Yours is a winery, not a software product — but the machine doesn’t care. It rewards the same things.

Where to start this week

  • Pull your tasting notes and tech sheets out of PDFs and images, and put them on the page as text.
  • Make your winery name, brands, and appellation identical everywhere you appear.
  • Check that your site (and your age-gate) isn’t blocking crawlers.
  • Add basic structured data so machines know you’re a winery.
